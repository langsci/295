\chapter{Non-concatenative verbal morphology} \label{chap:nonconcatenative}
While Japhug lacks tonal alternations like most Gyalrongic languages \citep{jackson05yingao, lai17khroskyabs, gong18these, zhangsy18stem}, non-concatenative segmental alternations are common.

This chapter briefly presents derivation onset alternations (¬ß\ref{sec:onset.alternations}), and then provides a detailed account of inflectional stem alternations (¬ß\ref{sec:stem.alternation}), vowel contraction (¬ß\ref{sec:contraction}) and partial reduplication (¬ß\ref{sec:redp.verb}) in verbal morphology.



\section{Onset alternations} \label{sec:onset.alternations}
\is{consonant!alternation}
While most of the prefixal derivation morphology in Japhug is fairly concatenative, a handful of prefixal morphological processes involve consonantal alternations. 

Prenasalization is found in the anticausative (¬ß\ref{sec:anticausative}) and a handful of frozen verb forms (¬ß\ref{sec:fossil.prenasalization}, ¬ß\ref{sec:caus.Z}).  In all cases, it is probably the result of the fusion of a nasal prefix with an unvoiced stop/affricative, turning it into the corresponding voiced prenasalized obstruent (¬ß\ref{sec:anticausative.direction}).

Another type of consonant alternation is the \forme{Œ≤-} \fl{} \forme{b-} fortition in the causative verb  \japhug{zbra Å}{attach together} (¬ß\ref{sec:sig.caus.irregular.other}).


\section{Stem alternations} \label{sec:stem.alternation}
\is{vowel!alternation} \is{stem alternation}
Northern Gyalrong languages have three verbal stems, labeled I, II and III following \citet{jackson00sidaba} (Situ dialects lack stem III, but some varieties have additional stems I' and II', see \citealt{zhangsy18stem}). Stem I is the default form, stem II mainly occurs in Aorist and Past Imperfective, and stem III occurs in the Non-Past \textsc{sg}\fl{}3 direct forms of some transitive verbs (¬ß\ref{sec:indexation.mixed}).

In Japhug, no verb has more than two different stems. Stem II is different from stem I in only a handful of irregular verbs (¬ß\ref{sec:stem2}), while stem III has been regularized (¬ß\ref{sec:stem3}).

The \forme{…£i/…£e} alternation has an exact correlate in Tshobdun (\forme{wi/w…õ î}, \citealt[175]{jackson00sidaba}). The verb \japhug{ti}{say} has an irregular correspondence with other Gyalrong languages, where an affricate onset is found (Tshobdun \forme{¬¥ts…ô} `say', \citealt[174]{jackson00sidaba}, Situ  \forme{ts…ôÃÇ, tsiÃÑs}, \citealt[318]{zhangsy18stem}). In Zbu however, a unique alternation between affricate and stop is found in this verb (stem I \forme{¬¥ts ∞…ô}, stem II \forme{¬¥t ∞it},  \citealt[225]{gong18these}). The correspondence between the Japhug stem II \forme{-t…Øt} and its Zbu equivalent \forme{¬¥t ∞it} is regular (apart from the aspiration). The irregular paradigm of this verb in Zbu certainly has to be reconstructed to proto-Gyalrong. Situ and Tshobdun have generalized the affricate to stem II, while Japhug has remade the stem I by generalizing the dental stop.

Suppletion is observed in the paradigm of \japhug{…ïe}{go}, whose stem II \forme{-ari} is perhaps related to the stem I of the verb `go' found in some dialects of Zbu (\forme{riÃÇ}, \citealt[274]{jackson04showu}). Xtokavian dialects of Japhug have \forme{t ∞…êl} as the stem II of this verb \citep{linluo03}, a form borrowed from Tibetan \tibet{‡Ωê‡Ω£‡ºã}{t ∞al}{go beyond, pass}.

The transitive verb \japhug{s…Øx…ïe}{send}, `let go' is the causative of  \japhug{…ïe}{go}, and its stem II presents an irregular vowel merger \forme{s…Ø…£+ari} $\rightarrow$ \forme{s…§…£ri}, instead of expected $\dagger$\forme{s…§ri} or $\dagger$\forme{as…Ø…£ri} (¬ß\ref{sec:caus.sAG}). Other derivations from \japhug{…ïe}{go} also have suppletion, but the alternations are regular. The autive and the vertitive \forme{n…Ø-} prefixes (¬ß\ref{sec:autobenefactive} , ¬ß\ref{sec:vertitive}) in \forme{n…Ø-…ïe} `go by oneself'/`go back' are predictably infixed (¬ß\ref{sec:inner.prefixal.chain}) within the stem II \forme{ari} as \forme{a<n…Ø>ri}  (see examples \ref{ex:Wrte.YAnWBde2} in ¬ß\ref{sec:autoben.lexicalized} and \ref{ex:tWZo.kW} in ¬ß\ref{sec:genr.pro}). the distributed action derivation \japhug{n…§…ï…Ø…ïe}{go around} likewise has the expected reduplicated stem \forme{-an…§r…Øri}.
 
The verb \japhug{n…Ø…£i}{come back}, vertitive of \japhug{…£i}{come}, shows the expected stem II \forme{n…Ø…£e} with vowel alternation. Its lexicalized causative \japhug{s…Ø…£e}{invite} on the other hand, has lost stem alternation, but appears to have generalized stem II (¬ß\ref{sec:sig.caus.irregular.other}).

Rather than analyzing the \forme{-m} suffix as a part of the stem III, it could alternatively be possible to view it as part of the suffixal chain. Under such an analysis, it would be located in slot +1 (¬ß\ref{sec:suffixes}). Bipartite verbs provide evidence against such an analysis however (¬ß\ref{sec:bipartite}).

\subsubsection{Backformation} \label{sec:stem3.backformation}
\is{backformation} \is{analogy!stem alternation}
An indirect consequence of the perfect regularity of stem III formation (¬ß\ref{sec:stem3.form}) is that in some rare cases, the stem I has been generated from the stem III by applying the alternations backwards.

The stem I of \japhug{k ∞o}{give}, `pass over' (¬ß\ref{sec:ditransitive.indirective}) regularly corresponds to Tshobdun \forme{¬¥k ∞i} (as if from proto-Gyalrong \forme{*k ∞a≈ã}). On the other hand, Zbu \forme{k ∞…êÃÇm, k ∞…ôÃÇm, ¬¥k ∞…ôm} `give' (\citealt[229]{gong18these}) and Tangut  \tangut{òìØ}{1105}{k ∞jow}{1.56} (\citealt[200--201]{jacques14esquisse}, proto-Tangut \forme{*khjVm}) match the Japhug stem III \forme{k ∞…§m}. A possible hypothesis to account for these diverging correspondences would be that Japhug and Tshobdun have preserved the original verb root, and that Zbu and Tangut have generalized stem III to the whole paradigm. However, there are three reasons why such an explanation is problematic. 

First, the verb \tangut{òìØ}{1105}{k ∞jow}{1.56} is highly irregular, and therefore unlikely to have been analogized. Second, Zbu is phylogenetically closer to Tshobdun and Japhug than it is to Tangut, and therefore shared features between Zbu and Tangut are more likely to be due to common retention than to common innovation. Third, there is Japhug-internal evidence that the stem I \forme{-o} is secondary.

The transitive verb \japhug{fkro}{put in order}, arrange', whose stem III is \forme{fkr…§m} is borrowed from the past tense of the Tibetan verb \tibet{‡Ω†‡ΩÇ‡æ≤‡Ω∫‡Ωò‡ºã‡Ωñ‡ΩÄ‡æ≤‡Ωò‡ºã}{‚Åøgrem-bkram}{spread out}. The stem I \forme{fkro} is clearly backformed from the stem III, by overapplication of the \forme{-o/-…§m} alternation. Some speakers treat this verb as having a non-alternating stem \forme{fkr…§m}, showing that backformation is still an ongoing process.

The case of \forme{fkro} offers a model to analyze Japhug \japhug{k ∞o}{give}, `pass over' and Tshobdun \forme{¬¥k ∞i} (\citealt[201]{jacques14esquisse}): this verb originally had a stem I \forme{*k ∞…êm} in proto-Gyalrong, preserved in Zbu, but underwent backformation to \forme{*k ∞a≈ã} in the common ancestor of Japhug and Tshobdun, subsequently evolving to \forme{k ∞o} and \forme{¬¥k ∞i} by the application of regular sound laws. This is one piece of evidence that Tshobdun is closer to Japhug than it is to Zbu.

The regular stem III \forme{Œ≤ze} of the verb \japhug{Œ≤zu}{make} does appear in some non-finite forms such as \forme{c ∞…Ø-k…Ø-Œ≤ze} in (\ref{ex:Wmat.chWkWBze}) (see also \ref{ex:YWkWnWBze} in ¬ß\ref{sec:autoben.spontaneous}), in the dummy transitive subject construction (¬ß\ref{sec:transitive.dummy}), meaning `grow' or in collocation with the noun \forme{…Ø-tsa} (in the meaning `be suitable', ¬ß\ref{sec:Bzu.lv}) for instance.

\section{Vowel contraction} \label{sec:contraction}
 \is{contracting verb} \is{vowel!contraction}
Contracting verbs have polysyllabic stems whose first syllable is \forme{a-}. All of these verbs are  morphologically intransitive (¬ß\ref{sec:transitivity.morphology}), but some of them can have semi-objects or oblique arguments (¬ß\ref{sec:semi.transitive}). The \forme{a-} element can be a valency-decrea\-sing prefix (including the passive  ¬ß\ref{sec:passive}, reciprocal (¬ß\ref{sec:reciprocal} or distributed property ¬ß\ref{sec:distributed.amW} prefixes), a denominal prefix (the stative denominal \forme{a-} ¬ß\ref{sec:denom.a} or a disyllabic denominal prefix with \forme{a-} as its first syllable, ¬ß\ref{sec:denom.contracting}), a deideophonic prefix (¬ß\ref{sec:a.nA.deidph}) or a synchronically non-analyzable element (as in \japhug{aro}{own} or \japhug{amdz…Ø}{sit}).
 
To represent vowel contraction in a straightforward way, the notation employed in this grammar separates the vowel of the preceding prefix and the contracting by a hyphen, the prefix is transcribed in its base form, and the result of the vowel contraction is indicated after the hyphen. For instance \forme{p…Ø-a-} is to be read as `prefix \forme{p…Ø-} merging with the contracting vowel as \ipa{pa}', while \forme{p…Ø-…§-} indicates merger of the preverb with the contracting vowel as \ipa{p…§}. This orthographic rule transcribes contracting vowels in several ways (\forme{-…§\trt}, \forme{-a-} or \forme{-o-}) depending on the prefix with which they merge (see \tabref{tab:preverb.contraction} below). It is important to note however that there is no contrast between \forme{-a\trt}, \forme{-…§-} and \forme{-o-} as contracting vowels: there are simply morphologically-conditioned allomorphs.\footnote{The \forme{a-} allomorph is used as citation form of contracting verbs. }
 
 This convention has the advantage of disambiguating verb forms that have become identical due to vowel contraction (for instance, \forme{t…§-…§-} vs. \forme{t…Ø-…§\trt}, both of which surface as \ipa{t…§}), and making the verb forms more easily parseable in the corpus.

\section{Partial reduplication in verbal morphology } \label{sec:redp.verb}
\is{reduplication!verb}
\subsection{Initial reduplication} \label{sec:verb.initial.redp}
Verb-initial reduplication has a number of morphosyntactic functions, involving clause linking (conditionals ¬ß\ref{sec:redp.protasis}, temporal clauses (¬ß\ref{sec:iterative.coincidence}), expressing increase of degree (¬ß\ref{sec:redp.gradual.increase}), totality (¬ß\ref{sec:totalitative.redp}) or temporal resilience (¬ß\ref{sec:emphatic.autive}). With the possible exception of its use in the protasis of conditionals, all of the functions of the initial reduplication present an iconic component typical of reduplication crosslinguistically. 

Initial reduplication belongs to inflectional morphology. The only cases of lexicalization involve grammaticalization of reduplicated finite verb form into a discourse marker  (see ¬ß\ref{sec:delimitative} and ¬ß\ref{sec:redp.protasis} below).
 
 %{sec:vowel.harmony}
 \subsubsection{Morphophonology} \label{sec:initial.redp.morpho}
While initial reduplication follows the general rules of partial reduplication (¬ß\ref{sec:partial.redp}), two additional rules have to be taken into account. 

First, the outcome of the reduplication of the negative prefix \forme{m…Ø-} is \forme{m…Ø\redp{}m…§-} with vowel alternation (¬ß\ref{sec:neg.allomorphs}), not distinguishable from that of the prefix \forme{m…§-}.

Second, when the initial syllable of the word contains more than one morpheme, as in the case of the \forme{…ï-} or \forme{ ë-} allomorphs of the translocative prefix combined with an orientation preverb (¬ß\ref{sec:translocative.morpho}), reduplication can disregard morpheme boundaries, resulting in a replication of the  two morphemes: \forme{…ï-k…§-ts ∞i-t-a} (\textsc{tral}-\textsc{aor}-drink-\textsc{pst}:\textsc{tr}-\textsc{1sg}) thus yields \forme{\rouge{…ï-k…Ø}\redp{}…ï-k…§-ts ∞i-t-a} as in  (\ref{ex:CkWCkAtshita}). \iai{Tshendzin} however also accepts reduplication of the first morpheme only, reduplicated with addition of \forme{-…Ø}  (\forme{\rouge{…ï…Ø}\redp{}…ï-k…§-ts ∞i-t-a}).
 
\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:CkWCkAtshita}

\end{exe}

Example (\ref{ex:CtWtAkWtshAt}) illustrates a third possible pattern, with reduplication of the orientation preverb \forme{…ï-t…§-k…Ø-ts ∞…§t} $\Rightarrow$ \forme{…ï-\rouge{t…Ø}\redp{}t…§-k…Ø-ts ∞…§t} without affecting the associated motion marker.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:CtWtAkWtshAt}
\gll k…Øki t…Ørme k…Øra …ï-t…Ø\redp{}t…§-k…Ø-ts ∞…§t  ëo n…Ø pj…§ÃÅ-w…£-sat-n…Ø. \\

\subsection{Verb stem reduplication as secondary exponence} \label{sec:verb.stem.redp}
\is{reduplication!secondary exponence}
Prefixal partial reduplication of the verb stem is a secondary exponence, used in combination with a prefix, in several productive verbal forms listed in \tabref{tab:verb.redp.prefix}. 

Stem reduplication occurs in two converbs, the gerund (¬ß\ref{sec:gerund} and the purposive converb (¬ß\ref{sec:purposive.converb}), together with a \forme{s…§(z)-} prefix cognate to that of the oblique participle (¬ß\ref{sec:oblique.participle}).
